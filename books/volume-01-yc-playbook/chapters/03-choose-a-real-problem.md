# Chapter 3 — Choose a Real Problem

> **Core Principle:** Begin with a painful situation you can observe, not a
> technology searching for a reason to exist.

## Learning Objectives

- Turn a broad idea into a specific problem statement.
- Evaluate frequency, consequence, current alternatives, and access.
- Select one problem for discovery without pretending the solution is proven.

## Deep Dive

“An AI assistant for everyone” describes a possible solution category. It does
not tell you whose work is failing, what happens, or why the failure matters.
A useful problem statement names a person, situation, desired outcome, current
workaround, and consequence.

YC’s proposed order of operations starts with a problem the founder cares about,
then moves to possible solutions and a small launch.[^order] Paul Graham’s essay
on startup ideas similarly recommends noticing gaps and problems, especially
ones the founders understand from experience.[^ideas] Neither source proves
that a familiar problem is a good business. Familiarity gives you a better
starting hypothesis and easier access to contradictory evidence.

Compare candidate problems with five filters:

- **Specific:** Can you describe the moment the problem appears?
- **Frequent:** Does it recur often enough to shape behavior?
- **Consequential:** Does it cost time, money, safety, trust, or opportunity?
- **Unresolved:** Are current alternatives slow, risky, expensive, or avoided?
- **Reachable:** Can you contact and observe relevant people now?

Do not add the scores and call the highest one “validated.” The comparison only
helps you choose where to investigate. A low-frequency problem can still matter
when its consequence is severe. A frequent annoyance may not justify change.

Write the problem without your product name: “When [user] is in [situation],
they struggle to [outcome]. They currently [alternative], which causes
[consequence].” Then ask users to correct it.

## AI Founder Interpretation

AI is good at expanding a list of possible users and workflows. That abundance
creates a risk: plausible text can look like market evidence. Treat every
AI-generated problem as a search lead, never as an observed fact.

Give AI de-identified interview notes and ask it to group repeated situations,
alternatives, and contradictions. Return to the original notes before deciding.
Do not upload private customer material without permission and safeguards.

## Callouts

### Decision Lens

> **Decision Lens:** Can you identify the last real occasion when this problem
> happened to someone you can contact?

### Common Failure

> **Common Failure:** Rewriting a product feature as a “pain point.” “People
> need automated summaries” is still a solution claim; name the failed outcome.

## Key Takeaways

- A technology category is not a problem statement.
- Familiarity and access improve discovery but do not prove demand.
- Frequency, consequence, alternatives, and reachability guide exploration.
- AI-generated possibilities remain assumptions until people and behavior
  support them.
