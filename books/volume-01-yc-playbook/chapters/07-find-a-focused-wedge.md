# Chapter 7 — Find a Focused Wedge

> **Core Principle:** Start where urgent need, user access, and a repeatable
> outcome overlap.

## Learning Objectives

- Define a narrow initial customer profile without shrinking the long-term
  vision.
- Compare candidate wedges using urgency, access, and learning density.
- Write an entry-point promise that can be tested with real users.

## Deep Dive

A wedge is the smallest market position from which you can learn and expand. It
is not “small companies” or “health care.” It combines a specific person,
situation, outcome, and route to reach them.

YC’s essential advice recommends focus and direct work with early customers,
while Paul Graham describes deliberately narrow markets and manual recruiting
as ways to get an early system moving.[^essential][^scale] FounderOS synthesis:
choose the segment where every conversation and delivery teaches you about the
same core workflow.

Score candidate wedges on urgency, access, repeated need, speed to value, and
adjacency. Adjacency asks whether success creates a credible path to nearby
users or workflows. Do not choose a wedge only because it has the highest
budget; long sales cycles can prevent early learning.

Write a narrow promise: “For [specific user] during [situation], we help achieve
[measurable outcome] without [important current cost].” Then test whether the
same language and delivery process work repeatedly.

## AI Founder Interpretation

AI can compare segment notes and suggest patterns. It cannot establish that a
group is reachable or urgent. Require named contacts, observed situations, and
a real outreach path before accepting a wedge recommendation.

Use synthetic examples only to rehearse. Do not count them as customer evidence.

## Callouts

### Decision Lens

> **Decision Lens:** In which segment can you personally reach ten relevant
> people and deliver one outcome within the next two weeks?

### Common Failure

> **Common Failure:** Keeping the segment broad to protect ambition. Broadness
> usually creates different problems, messages, and product requirements at once.

## Checklist

- [ ] Define three candidate wedges with user, situation, and outcome.
- [ ] Assess urgency, access, repetition, speed, and adjacency.
- [ ] Name a direct route to ten relevant people.
- [ ] Select one wedge for a time-boxed test.
- [ ] Record the evidence that would justify expansion.

## Key Takeaways

- A focused wedge concentrates learning; it does not cap the vision.
- Reachability and speed can matter more than theoretical budget at the start.
- Expansion should follow repeatable value, not founder impatience.
- AI suggestions require real contacts and observed workflows.
